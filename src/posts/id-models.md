## ADDIE vs. Layers of Necessity Model

I selected the Wedman and Tessmer model (Layers of Necessity) for comparison. A key observation from their work is that **"ID models are more alike than different"** (1991, p. 48).

The ADDIE model encompasses five phases: Analyze, Design, Develop, Implement, and Evaluate. Most comparable models (ASSURE, Dick and Carey, Hannafin and Peck, Knick and Gustafson) follow similar structures with minor variations.

The Wedman and Tessmer approach differs significantly by layering multiple design models atop one another, emphasizing efficiency over effectiveness, explicitly permitting non-sequential task completion, and reflecting the "messy nature" of instructional design.

The **first layer** creates a "minimum viable product" incorporating ADDIE stages — situational assessment, goal analysis, instructional strategy development, materials development, and evaluation/revision. This resembles rapid prototyping. **Subsequent layers** add refinements such as instruction enhancements, strategies, manuals, assessments, media materials, and complex tools as time permits.

This model **"bridges the gulf between theory and practice"** (1991, p. 52), and I've seen similar frameworks in my own experience developing OER-based textbooks.

## Why Instructional Design Models Matter

Sure, you might end up in the right place without the map or by relying on your best judgment, but using the map will be more effective.

As a first-year teacher, I felt overwhelmed managing classroom complexity. Emphasis on "Students Will Be Able To" (SWBAT) statements provided focus — clarifying what students should actually accomplish rather than spinning wheels on irrelevant tasks.

A systematic ID process provides a roadmap keeping end goals visible, recursive flexibility for revisiting earlier stages, structure and purpose throughout design, and project management support.
